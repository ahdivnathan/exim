Problem¶

International trade policy and its consequences have long been a contentious issue in economics. After World War II, industrialized nations began a reversal of the previously protectionist era of trade policy which expanded to developing countries through the following decades. By the 1990's increasingly liberal global trade policy had been established including Asia, South America, and Eastern Europe (to a lesser extent Africa and the Middle East). However, the effects of this liberalization have been mixed and at times underperformed expectations. Of the many, one important consequence of trade liberalization is trade diversity (of international trade partners and of products).1 With our project we attempt to assess the influence of this diversity on macroeconomic performance in the last 18 years.

To develop a singular metric of trade diversity, we constructed a Trade Diversity Index (TDI). We calculated the variances of trade concentration for each nation's products and international partners across both imports and exports respectively. The average of these four variances is the country's TDI value. As a proxy for macroeconomic performance we used stock indices, exchange rates, and GDP growth among others. We utilized many sources in order to collect such a variety and depth of data including the BACI International Trade Database, the Center for Economic Complexity, the World Bank, and Yahoo Finance.

The time-lag between trade diversity changes and a measurable economic result can be a confounding factor in these analysis. To account for this we perform both a matched-year regression across our variables of interest as well as a lagged regression comparing the TDI with economic indicators both before and after the TDI date.

Difficulties with Data¶

Performing a global economic analysis involving multiple variables requires working with a very large sets of data gathered in different mannners from various sources. This process presents the complication of standardization. Certain macroeconomic indicators are not well tracked in many nations, many international stock indices (particularly of smaller nations) did not seem to retain easily accessible historical data, and finding historical exchange rate data turned out to be far more tedious than expected. Currency changes, currency manipulations, and sovereignty issues all contributed to conflicts between trade policy and currency usage. Thus there was potential for incomplete and misrepresentative data. In the end, after many hours scouring sources for more reliable and more robust sets of data, we were able to consolidate a data set of ten macroeconomic indicators over 17 years across 200 nations.

The goal of gathering and cleaning was to store all data in R Data Frames for later analysis. This process also took significant time in order to ensure complete congruity between dataframe formats that is vital for an accurate regression. Further obscuring this process was the fact each observation for our regressions of TDI data on our macroeconomic indicators had three identifying parameters that had to be perfectly indexed for both data sets: the variable measured, the year, and the country. As such we had to be meticulous with our pre-analysis cleaning and setup of data.

Finally, our variables of interest (trade diversity and macroeconomic performance) are results of such an immense group of economic, political, and social factors that ultimately there were sub-trends within our data that needed to be addressed in a more nuanced manner. We used regression and correlation analysis as well as k-means clustering analysis to attempt to identify and control for these factors.

Such potential for omitted variable bias is always an issue and we did our best to create the TDI in a manner to best avoid it. In the end we used the variance method described below.

Presentation of Findings¶

Trade Diversification Index¶

To begin, we created a Trade Diversification Index (TDI) in order to gauge the diversity of trade for a given country in a given year. Our goal in this project was to analyze how a country's TDI affects its macroeconomic performance. The TDI was calculated as follows:
1. For a particular year, calculate the percentage of a country's products imported from each product category. Call this the "Products Imported" variable. We will look up the product categories using the Harmonized Trade Classification System (HS4).
2. Calculate the variance of the Products Imported variable.
3. Complete steps (1) and (2) for products exported, import partners, and export partners to find variance of Products Exported, variance of Import Partners, and variance of Export Partners. Import and Export Partners were classified by continent.
4. Take the average of the 4 variances to create the TDI. A low TDI therefore implies that the country's trade is well diversified, while a high TDI implies that the country's trade is concentrated in some way that causes it to be poorly diversified.

Regression Analysis¶

In order to understand how a country's diversity of trade affects its macroeconomic performance, we regressed the following variables on TDI:
1. Exchange Rate
2. Stock index returns
3. CO2 Emissions
4. Exports Growth
5. GDP Growth
6. Gross Domestic Savings
7. Net Migration
8. Infant Mortality
9. Real Interest Rate

As we see here, the regression coefficient of -0.23586 on TDI, which is stored as "indVariable", implies that for every 1-point increase in a country's TDI, its spot rate with the USD (Foreign/USD) decreases by 0.23 units of the foreign currency. We generated the following scatterplot to demonstrate the relationship between trade diversity and exchange rate. As can be seen, there is a clear negative correlation between the two variables, indicating that as a country's trading operations become less diverse, its currency actually becomes more powerful relative to the US Dollar. This does not follow from our intuition, as we would expect countries with more diverse trading operations to have better-performing currencies. However, it may also be true that there is a variable that is linked to both less diverse trading operations and a more powerful currency. This may occur if, for example, an authoritarian government heavily subsidizes and supports certain industries while neglecting others, and simultaneously artificially buoys its currency to compete in the international market. Additionally, countries may peg their exchange rate to another currency or a commodity price (i.e. gold or silver), causing the currency to be positively correlated with other variables that may be negatively influenced by increased trade diversity.

Unfortunately, the relationships between TDI and the other macroeconomic variables were either not significant or were very small to the point that they had negligible effects on macroeconomic performance. Therefore, we began thinking more qualitatively about other trends we might see in a relationship between TDI and macroeconomic variables. One of the areas that we explored was the possibility of a lagged effect. In order to investigate this, we lagged TDI by 2 years before regressing the macroeconomic variables on it. We also looked into the possibility of the other macroeconomic variables affecting trade diversity, for which we lagged the macroeconomic indicator by 1 or 2 years before regressing it on trade diversity. Based on our qualitative analysis of the variables and examining Pearson correlation coefficients between the macroeconomic variables and the TDI, we decided the appropriate lag to apply in order to conduct the regression analysis.

Stock Index Returns: 2-year lag (TDI affects future returns)
CO2 Emissions: 2-year lag (TDI affects future emissions)
Exports Growth: Matched-year (TDI affects immediate growth)
GDP Growth: Matched-year (TDI affects immediate growth)
Gross Domestic Saving: 2-year lag (TDI affects future saving)
Infant Mortality: 2-year lag (TDI affects future mortality rate)
Net Migration: 2-year lag on migration (Net migration affects future TDI)
Real Interest Rate: 2-year lag on real interest rate (Real interest rate affects future TDI)
Exchange Rate: Matched-year (TDI affects immediate exchange rate)

To illustrate how we went about examining the appropriate lags for each of these variables, we have included the correlation results from our investigation of net migration below. Here, prepVectors conducts a matched-year correlation, prepVectors2 lags TDI by 2 years, prepVectors3 lags Net Migration by 2 years, and prepVectors4 lags Net Migration by 1 year. As we can see, the most significant correlation is seen when Net Migration is lagged by 2 years, which may be caused by influxes of immigrants leading to entepreneurial efforts and the eventual emergence of new businesses. The results of this correlation may be interpreted as follows: An increase in net migration implies that there will be an eventual increase in a country's diversity of trade.

We see here that we can see some general economic development trends by clustering countries based on their TDI progression from 1995 to 2011. In cluster 2 (Green Line), the least diverse cluster, we have many countries that have experienced wars in the recent past, many territories which are subject to another nation's economic and trade policy, and smaller countries who do not have many resources to develop. For example, we have many countries in this cluster who were involved in the Yugoslav Wars, and many smaller territories who are subject to the trade policies of another nation and may be exploited due to the parent nation's trade objectives.

Another interesting trend is shown by the contrast between the red and cyan lines (clusters 1 and 4, respectively). We see in cluster 1 that the mean TDI decreases by more than 100 points over the 17 years under observation, while the mean TDI of cluster 4 increases drastically over the observation period. In cluster 1, we have many countries who have risen to international prominence over the last two decades, including Mexico, the Philippines, Vietnam, and Singapore, among others. On the other hand, cluster 4 contains many countries that have been struggling to develop in the recent past, such as Chad, Djibouti, Somalia, North Korea, and Sudan. Overall, we do see a relationship between the cluster that a country is in and the country's economic performance over the last two decades.
